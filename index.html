Ciliary Biology

Comprehensive content from Ross & Pawlina's Histology: A Text and Atlas, Alberts Molecular Biology of the Cell, and Junqueira's Basic Histology.

Overview of Cilia

Cilia are slender, hair-like projections of the plasma membrane that extend from the apical surface of many epithelial cells. They are among the most architecturally complex organelles in the body, measuring approximately 5–15 μm in length and 250 nm in diameter, and are found on respiratory epithelium, the oviduct, the ependyma of the brain ventricles, and many other surfaces.

Ross MH, Pawlina W. Histology: A Text and Atlas. 8th ed. Philadelphia: Wolters Kluwer; 2020. Chapter 5: Epithelial Tissue, pp. 112–130.

Classification of Cilia

Cilia are broadly classified into two functional categories:

  • Motile cilia (9+2): Contain the classic 9+2 axonemal arrangement. Actively beat to propel fluid or mucus across epithelial surfaces. Found on respiratory epithelium (trachea, bronchi), oviduct, ependymal cells, and the embryonic node.
  • Primary cilia (9+0): Lack the central pair of microtubules and dynein arms. Largely non-motile; function as sensory antennae. Found on virtually every nucleated cell in the body. Critical for Hedgehog, Wnt, and PDGF signalling pathways.
Satir P, Christensen ST. Overview of structure and function of mammalian cilia. Annu Rev Physiol. 2007;69:377–400.

Ultrastructure: The Axoneme

The axoneme is the internal cytoskeletal core of a cilium, composed of microtubules and associated proteins. In motile cilia the arrangement is designated 9+2:

  • Nine outer doublets: Each doublet consists of an A-tubule (13 protofilaments, complete) fused to a B-tubule (10 protofilaments, incomplete, sharing three with A). A-tubules carry the outer and inner dynein arms and radial spoke heads.
  • Central pair: Two singlet microtubules enclosed by a central sheath, projecting radial spokes toward the outer doublets. The central pair rotates during ciliary beating, coordinating doublet sliding.
  • Nexin–dynein regulatory complex (N-DRC): Elastic links connecting adjacent outer doublets, converting inter-doublet sliding into ciliary bending.

The Basal Body

Each cilium is anchored to the cell by a basal body (formerly called a kinetosome), a modified centriole approximately 400 nm in length. Its key features are:

  • Triplet microtubules: The basal body contains nine sets of microtubule triplets (A, B, and C tubules) arranged in a pinwheel. The C-tubule is absent above the transitional zone, giving rise to the doublet pattern of the axoneme.
  • Alar sheets (transitional fibres): Electron-dense Y-shaped fibres that connect the A-tubule of each doublet to the overlying plasma membrane at the base of the cilium, forming the ciliary gate.
  • Basal foot: A conical appendage projecting laterally from the basal body in the direction of the ciliary beat, determining polarity of beating across the epithelial surface.
  • Striated rootlet: A striated fibrous structure extending from the proximal end of the basal body deep into the cytoplasm; anchors the basal body and may transmit mechanical forces to the cell interior.
Gomperts BN, Gong-Cooper X, Hackett BP. Foxj1 regulates basal body anchoring to the cytoskeleton of ciliated pulmonary epithelial cells. J Cell Sci. 2004;117:1329–1337.

Ciliary Motility: The Sliding Filament Mechanism

Ciliary beating is produced by dynein-mediated sliding of adjacent doublets, constrained to bending by nexin links and the basal body anchor. The cycle proceeds in three phases:

  1. Power stroke: Outer dynein arms on doublets 1–4 (one hemisphere) are activated, causing those doublets to slide toward the ciliary tip relative to doublets 5–9. Nexin links constrain this to a bending motion. The cilium sweeps through ~30° in ~30 ms at maximum speed.
  2. Recovery stroke: The cilium returns to upright position with a slower, curved path so that minimal fluid is dragged back.
  3. Coordination (metachronal wave): Adjacent cilia beat slightly out of phase, producing a wave that efficiently transports mucus at 5–20 mm/min in the airway.
Force = n × fdynein × L ≈ 2–4 pN per dynein arm × 2,000 arms/cilium ≈ 4–8 nN

Intraflagellar Transport (IFT)

Because cilia lack ribosomes, all ciliary proteins must be transported from the cell body along the axoneme by intraflagellar transport. IFT is bidirectional:

  • IFT-B (anterograde): Driven by kinesin-2 toward the ciliary tip; delivers tubulin, dynein arms, and signalling proteins.
  • IFT-A (retrograde): Driven by cytoplasmic dynein-2 back to the basal body; recycles IFT machinery and removes turnover products.

⚕️ Clinical Significance: Kartagener Syndrome

Kartagener syndrome (a subset of Primary Ciliary Dyskinesia) is characterised by the triad of bronchiectasis, sinusitis, and situs inversus totalis (mirror-image organ arrangement). It results from autosomal-recessive mutations affecting dynein arm assembly (most commonly DNAI1 or DNAH5), rendering cilia immotile. Because the embryonic node cilia are responsible for establishing left–right body asymmetry, their absence causes random organ lateralisation in 50% of PCD cases.

Ciliogenesis

During differentiation of multiciliated cells (e.g. airway epithelium), cells produce hundreds of cilia via two pathways:

  • Intracellular pathway: Centrioles are generated de novo from deuterosomes — electron-dense platforms in the cytoplasm — and then migrate to the apical membrane where they mature into basal bodies.
  • Extracellular pathway: In some cell types, nascent basal bodies dock at the apical membrane before cilia extend, guided by rab8 and centrin proteins.
Spassky N, Meunier A. The development and functions of multiciliated epithelia. Nat Rev Mol Cell Biol. 2017;18(7):423–436.

Ciliopathies & Research

Cutting-edge research in ciliary biology — from genetic disease mechanisms to therapeutic strategies and developmental signalling.

Ciliopathies: Disease of Defective Cilia

The term ciliopathy describes a growing class of genetic disorders caused by dysfunction of cilia. Given that primary cilia are present on nearly every cell, ciliopathies are remarkably pleiotropic — affecting the kidney, retina, brain, liver, skeleton, and laterality organs simultaneously.

1. Primary Ciliary Dyskinesia (PCD)

PCD affects approximately 1 in 15,000–20,000 live births worldwide. Over 50 causative genes have been identified, encoding proteins involved in outer dynein arm assembly (ODA-DC), inner dynein arm assembly (DNAAF proteins), radial spoke heads, and the central pair.

2. Polycystic Kidney Disease (PKD) and Renal Ciliopathies

Primary cilia on renal tubular epithelial cells act as flow sensors, bending in response to urine flow and triggering Ca²⁺ signalling through PC1 (polycystin-1) and PC2 (polycystin-2) channel complexes. Mutations in PKD1 or PKD2 abrogate this mechanosensation, leading to uncontrolled cell proliferation and cyst formation. ADPKD affects ~1 in 400–1,000 individuals, making it one of the most common monogenic diseases in humans.

3. Hedgehog Signalling and Primary Cilia

Primary cilia are essential hubs for vertebrate Hedgehog (Hh) signalling. In the absence of Sonic Hedgehog (Shh) ligand, the receptor Patched1 (PTCH1) resides in the ciliary membrane and suppresses Smoothened (SMO). Shh binding removes PTCH1, allowing SMO entry into cilia, where it activates GLI transcription factors at the ciliary tip. Disruption of this pathway causes holoprosencephaly, polydactyly, and Gorlin syndrome (basal cell naevus syndrome).

⚕️ Therapeutic Horizons

Read-through therapy: ~30% of PCD patients carry nonsense mutations amenable to ataluren (PTC124)-mediated read-through, restoring partial dynein arm expression. Phase II trials are ongoing.

Trikafta-inspired approach: Small-molecule chaperones (analogous to CFTR correctors) designed to rescue misfolded DNAAF proteins are in pre-clinical development for dynein assembly factor mutations.

SMO inhibitors: Vismodegib and sonidegib (FDA-approved) block the ciliary SMO pathway to treat Gorlin syndrome and locally advanced basal cell carcinoma.

4. Cilia in Left–Right Asymmetry

The embryonic node (a transient pit at the ventral surface of the developing embryo) contains ~200 monocilia that rotate clockwise at ~600 rpm, generating a leftward flow of morphogen-containing nodal vesicular parcels (NVPs). This flow activates Nodal signalling preferentially on the left side, establishing the left–right body axis. Immotile node cilia act as mechanosensors detecting this flow. Failure of node cilia produces heterotaxia — randomised organ placement.
